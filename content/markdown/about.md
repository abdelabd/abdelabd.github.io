I'm broadly interested in the modeling and control of complex systems.

In high-energy physics (HEP), this mainly refers to particle detectors like ATLAS and CMS. I'm currently a [WATCHEP](https://watchep.org) Fellow, for which I'm working on two computational HEP projects. 

The first is the development of a PyTorch-based, differentiable version of the Delphes simulation software; this will allow researchers to automatically tune simulation parameters (e.g. tracking efficiency, momentum resolution) in phenomenology studies in order to better explain observed data. 

The other project is the development of an unfolding algorithm that (a) uses an iterative, likelihood-based unfolding procedure like that of [OmniFold](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.09107) to account for simulation bias, and (b) unlike OmniFold, uses a generative model to directly produce an unfolded/augmented dataset which automatically reproduces the true distributions, rather than just a set of weights.

I also design FPGA algorithms for the [ATLAS](https://atlas.cern/) detector at CERN, mainly for the low-latency readout of data from the Pixel subdetector, which is responsible for charged-particle tracking. In the past, I published a [feasibility study](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/big-data/articles/10.3389/fdata.2022.828666/full) on the use of graph neural networks on FPGAs for charged-particle tracking at ATLAS and CMS. 

On the materials-science side of things, "complex systems" refers specifically to *thin-film deposition* systems (e.g. pulsed laser deposition), which are a family of material growth techniques that work by iteratively depositing high-energy plasma, atomic, or molecular plumes on top of one another until they reach a desired thickness. As part of the [FastML for RHEED](https://github.com/abdelabd/CoaxlinkOcto_1cam_2404) project, I've led the design and development of an FPGA-accelerated ML algorithm for real-time, in-situ characterization of materials as they are being grown via thin-film deposition. This design will enable researchers to study and control the highly energetic and non-equilibrium dynamics of thin-film deposition.
